# ARKANOID — REVERSE ENGINEERING GAME DESIGN DOCUMENT

## 1. Game Identity

**Title:** Arkanoid
**Developer/Publisher:** Taito
**North America:** distributed by Romstar
**Release:** 1986 arcade
**Genre:** Block breaker / paddle action
**Primary play style:** Single-screen stage clearing with reflex control, angle manipulation, risk-reward power-up routing, and score optimization. ([Wikipedia][1])

## 2. Core High Concept

The player controls the **Vaus**, a paddle-like spacecraft at the bottom of the screen, and keeps a ball in play while destroying bricks arranged in a single-screen formation. The stage ends when all required destructible bricks are cleared or when the player uses a Break capsule to exit early through a side gate. The design expands Breakout by adding varied brick behaviors, falling capsules, enemies, stronger control expression through bounce angles, and a more dramatic audiovisual identity. ([StrategyWiki][2])

## 3. Reverse-Engineering Goal

The goal of a faithful reverse-engine is to reproduce:

* Spinner-based analog paddle movement
* Ball rebound behavior based on contact zones on the Vaus
* Brick durability rules
* Falling capsule logic and effect replacement rules
* Enemy interference patterns
* Score, lives, stage progression, and bonus structure
* Arcade pacing, including the increasing tension from ball speed and narrow recovery windows. ([StrategyWiki][2])

---

# 4. Player Fantasy

The player is not just “bouncing a ball.” They are:

* Piloting a responsive sci-fi paddle craft
* Controlling trajectories with precision
* Recovering from near-fail moments
* Chasing favorable capsule chains
* Turning a defensive game into an offensive one through Laser, Catch, Disruption, and Break routing. ([StrategyWiki][2])

The emotional arc is:
**calm control → rising chaos → clutch recovery → mastery through angle control.**

# 5. Cabinet / Hardware / Controls

## 5.1 Cabinet Form

Arkanoid shipped as a conversion/new game package through Romstar in North America, with installation materials for marquee, side art, control panel setup, wiring, and PCB mounting. The manual also references DIP-switch configuration and demo-with-sound behavior for operator setup. ([ARC ARC][3])

## 5.2 Primary Controls

* **Spinner / paddle control:** analog rotational input moves the Vaus left/right
* **1 button:**

  * Launch/release ball when held by Catch
  * Fire lasers when Laser mode is active
* **1P / 2P start**
* **Coin inputs** ([StrategyWiki][2])

## 5.3 Control Feel Requirements

A faithful clone must preserve:

* Immediate lateral response
* Fine low-latency tracking
* Ability to “sweep” for saves
* High precision near edges for shallow rebounds
* A sense that advanced players can intentionally aim. ([StrategyWiki][2])

# 6. Playfield Layout

## 6.1 Screen Composition

Single-screen fixed playfield:

* Brick field occupies upper/middle screen
* Vaus moves only on the bottom horizontal band
* Ball bounces between walls, bricks, and Vaus
* Side gate may open with Break effect
* Enemies descend through play space as moving interference
* HUD tracks score and lives/status. ([StrategyWiki][2])

## 6.2 Boundaries

* Left/right walls rebound ball
* Top wall rebounds ball
* Bottom edge is fail/life loss if the last active ball falls through
* Gold bricks may remain and do not block stage completion because they are indestructible and not counted toward clear condition. ([StrategyWiki][2])

---

# 7. Core Gameplay Loop

1. Start stage
2. Ball launches into brick formation
3. Player tracks ball and positions Vaus
4. Ball destroys bricks and may trigger a falling capsule
5. Player chooses whether to catch capsule or let it fall
6. Enemies may interfere
7. Ball speed and angle pressure increase over time
8. Stage clears when all required destructible bricks are removed, or early through Break exit
9. Repeat until lives are exhausted or game is completed. ([StrategyWiki][2])

---

# 8. Vaus Mechanics

## 8.1 Base Form

The Vaus is the player paddle. It slides horizontally only. Its main job is to keep the ball alive and redirect it into useful attack angles. ([StrategyWiki][2])

## 8.2 Bounce Zoning

A crucial reverse-engineering feature is that the outgoing angle depends on where the ball hits the Vaus:

* **Center/silver area:** sharp rebound angle
* **Red side bands:** about 45-degree rebound
* **Extreme edges:** shallow rebound angle

This is one of the main skill systems in Arkanoid and must feel deliberate rather than random. ([StrategyWiki][2])

## 8.3 Transform States

The Vaus can change into distinct temporary functional states:

* **Normal**
* **Enlarged**
* **Laser**
* **Catch**

Only one active power-up state is generally in force at a time, with Disruption being a special multi-ball exception in gameplay behavior. ([StrategyWiki][2])

---

# 9. Ball Physics and Behavior

## 9.1 Ball Role

The ball is the primary attack object. It destroys bricks, triggers capsules, collides with enemies, and determines most stage tempo. ([StrategyWiki][2])

## 9.2 Fundamental Properties

The ball must support:

* Position and velocity vector
* Wall rebounds
* Brick collision and destruction
* Vaus rebound logic based on impact zone
* Optional catch-on-contact in Catch mode
* Split into three simultaneous balls in Disruption mode. ([StrategyWiki][2])

## 9.3 Speed Behavior

Orange **Slow** capsules reduce ball velocity, and multiple slows stack. However, the ball gradually speeds up again as it continues bouncing and destroying bricks, with occasional sudden-feeling increases. That acceleration pressure is important to the original feel. ([StrategyWiki][2])

## 9.4 Multi-Ball

Disruption creates **three balls total**. The player only loses a life when the final remaining active ball is lost. While multiple balls remain in play, new colored capsules do not fall. ([StrategyWiki][2])

# 10. Brick System

## 10.1 Brick Categories

Arkanoid uses three broad brick categories:

### Colored bricks

* Standard destructible bricks
* One hit to destroy
* Different colors give different point values

### Silver bricks

* Multi-hit bricks
* Hits required increase as stage count rises
* Score value scales with stage number

### Gold bricks

* Indestructible
* Not required for stage clear. ([StrategyWiki][2])

## 10.2 Colored Brick Point Values

Standard colored bricks award values from **50 to 120 points**, depending on color. ([StrategyWiki][2])

## 10.3 Silver Brick Scaling

Silver bricks take more than one hit. Early on they take **two hits**, and the required hit count increases by **one every eight stages**. Their point value is **50 × stage number**. ([StrategyWiki][2])

## 10.4 Gold Brick Function

Gold bricks act as permanent geometry:

* Route blockers
* Angle shapers
* Safe “walls” for ricochet design
* Difficulty modifiers within the stage pattern. ([StrategyWiki][2])

# 11. Capsule / Power-Up System

## 11.1 General Rules

Power-up capsules:

* Appear after a random number of **non-silver** bricks are destroyed
* Fall downward slowly
* Only one capsule appears at a time
* Must be collected by the Vaus to activate
* Normally persist until replaced by another power-up or until the current ball is lost. ([StrategyWiki][2])

## 11.2 Power-Up List

### L — Laser (red)

Transforms Vaus into laser mode.
Player can fire lasers upward with the button.
Lasers damage enemies and all bricks except gold; silver still require their normal total number of hits. ([StrategyWiki][2])

### E — Enlarge (blue)

Extends Vaus width, improving recovery and making capsule catches easier. ([StrategyWiki][2])

### C — Catch (green)

Ball sticks to the Vaus on contact.
Player presses fire to release it; ball also auto-releases after a delay.
This allows controlled aiming and tempo reset. ([StrategyWiki][2])

### S — Slow (orange)

Reduces ball speed.
Multiple Slow capsules stack.
Ball later ramps back up as play continues. ([StrategyWiki][2])

### B — Break (violet)

Creates a breakout exit on the right side.
Entering it skips to the next stage and awards a **10,000-point bonus**. ([StrategyWiki][2])

### D — Disruption (cyan)

Splits the ball into three active balls.
No new capsules drop while more than one ball is active. ([StrategyWiki][2])

### P — Player (gray)

Awards an extra life/Vaus. ([StrategyWiki][2])

## 11.3 Design Importance

The capsule system is the major source of run variance and strategic adaptation. A faithful remake should preserve the feeling that:

* Some capsules are immediate survival tools
* Some are score/routing tools
* Some can be situationally bad or risky
* Catching everything blindly is not always optimal. ([StrategyWiki][2])

---

# 12. Enemy System

Enemies drift into the playfield and move downward toward the bottom. They do not directly damage the Vaus on contact; instead, they interfere with ball travel and add clutter, timing disruption, and rebound unpredictability. In Laser mode they become shootable targets. ([StrategyWiki][2])

For reverse engineering, enemies should be treated as:

* Moving collision bodies for the ball
* Non-lethal to Vaus on direct contact
* Supplemental pressure, not the core win condition. ([StrategyWiki][2])

---

# 13. Lives, Failure, and Recovery

## 13.1 Life Loss

A life is lost when the last active ball falls below the Vaus and leaves play. ([StrategyWiki][2])

## 13.2 Recovery State

After life loss:

* Stage layout remains in its current damaged state
* Ball/Vaus reset for relaunch
* Temporary power state does not persist in the same way as full-stage reset
* Player resumes under reduced resources.

This persistence of stage damage is a major part of Arkanoid’s tension curve and comeback design. The exact restart feel must be preserved even if internal implementation differs. This behavior is consistent with documented gameplay descriptions and the game’s arcade structure. ([StrategyWiki][2])

## 13.3 Extra Lives

Extra lives come from **P capsules**; operator settings may also affect baseline life count and difficulty through DIP switches. The manual explicitly references tailoring game duration with DIP switches. ([StrategyWiki][2])

# 14. Scoring

## 14.1 Core Sources

Score is built from:

* Destroying colored bricks
* Destroying silver bricks
* Other incidental enemy/power interactions
* Break capsule stage skip bonus. ([StrategyWiki][2])

## 14.2 Known Values

* Colored bricks: **50–120 points** by color
* Silver bricks: **50 × current stage number**
* Break exit bonus: **10,000 points**. ([StrategyWiki][2])

## 14.3 Scoring Philosophy

Arkanoid score play rewards:

* Efficient destruction routes
* Risky ball angle management
* Maximizing brick value before using Break
* Leveraging multi-ball and Laser for faster cleanup. ([StrategyWiki][2])

# 15. Stage Structure

## 15.1 Stage Format

Each stage is a single screen with a distinct brick pattern. The game is famous for handcrafted arrangements rather than procedural layouts. Taito’s developers reportedly sketched stage designs on paper before implementation. ([Wikipedia][1])

## 15.2 Clear Rule

A stage clears when all required destructible bricks are removed. Gold bricks do not count against completion. A Break capsule can also allow early exit by sending the ball through the opened right-side gate. ([StrategyWiki][2])

## 15.3 Difficulty Progression

Difficulty rises through:

* Tighter brick arrangements
* More punishing rebound corridors
* More silver durability
* Faster effective pace
* Harder recovery paths
* Greater enemy interference pressure. ([StrategyWiki][2])

---

# 16. Difficulty Model

Arkanoid difficulty is not just “ball gets faster.” It is built from several overlapping systems:

* Smaller safe recovery windows
* Higher angle precision demand
* Brick durability escalation
* More dangerous geometry
* Capsule luck versus capsule discipline
* Increased punishment for positional drift. ([StrategyWiki][2])

A faithful clone should preserve the way the game swings between:

* controllable,
* hectic,
* then suddenly lethal.

# 17. Audio-Visual Style

## 17.1 Visual Identity

Arkanoid uses a futuristic neon sci-fi style, with geometric enemies and a spacecraft-themed paddle. The development background specifically cites **Tron** as visual inspiration. ([Wikipedia][1])

## 17.2 Visual Priorities for Reverse Engineering

Must preserve:

* High contrast brick readability
* Clear capsule letter/icon recognition
* Immediate ball visibility
* Distinct Vaus transform silhouettes
* Clean stage geometry legibility under high speed. ([Wikipedia][1])

## 17.3 Sound

The game’s music was created by **Hisayoshi Ogura**. In arcade operation, the manual references demo-with-sound settings, indicating attract-mode sound configuration mattered in cabinet presentation. ([Wikipedia][1])

# 21. Collision Priorities

A practical faithful-order collision model should handle:

1. Ball vs walls
2. Ball vs bricks
3. Ball vs enemies
4. Ball vs Vaus
5. Laser vs bricks/enemies
6. Vaus vs capsules
7. Ball vs Break gate

The most important requirement is deterministic-feeling rebound and no visually unfair tunneling at speed. This is an implementation inference based on the documented mechanics. ([StrategyWiki][2])

# 22. Game Feel Requirements

To feel like Arkanoid, the remake must get these right:

* Spinner movement must feel exact
* Edge hits must produce shallow “saving” or “routing” shots
* Ball speed changes must feel dangerous, not random
* Laser must feel powerful but not trivialize gold/silver rules
* Catch must enable intentional shot setup
* Multi-ball must feel chaotic but survivable
* Capsules must be readable and desirable instantly. ([StrategyWiki][2])

If these feel wrong, the game can be mechanically complete but still not feel like Arkanoid.

# 24. QA Checklist for Reverse Engineering

## 24.1 Mandatory Behavior Tests

* Does center/edge Vaus impact materially change rebound angle?
* Do colored bricks break in one hit?
* Do gold bricks never break?
* Do silver bricks scale hits over stage progression?
* Do capsules fall one at a time?
* Does Disruption suppress further capsule drops while multi-ball remains?
* Does Break open the right-side exit and award 10,000 points?
* Does Catch hold then release properly?
* Does Laser obey gold/silver limitations?
* Do enemies interfere with the ball and die to lasers?
* Does life loss occur only when the last active ball is lost? ([StrategyWiki][2])

## 24.2 Cabinet/Arcade Presentation Tests

* Attract loop works
* Coin/start flow works
* Demo sound option works
* Difficulty/lives options configurable
* Control sensitivity supports spinner-style precision.
