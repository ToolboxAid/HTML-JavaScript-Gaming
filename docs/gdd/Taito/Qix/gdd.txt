# QIX — REVERSE ENGINEERING GAME DESIGN DOCUMENT

---

# 1. Game Identity

**Title:** Qix
**Developer/Publisher:** Taito America
**Release:** 1981 (Arcade)
**Genre:** Abstract action / territory control
**Core Idea:** Claim territory by drawing lines while avoiding enemies and protecting the vulnerable “drawing cursor.”

---

# 2. High Concept

The player draws lines from the border of a rectangular playfield to **capture area** while avoiding:

* The chaotic line entity (**Qix**)
* Edge-traveling enemies (**Sparx**)
* Later-stage roaming enemies (**Fuse**)

The goal is to reach a **target percentage of captured area** (typically ~75%) to clear the level.

---

# 3. What Makes Qix Unique

Unlike most arcade games:

* No fixed player character sprite
* No shooting (primary gameplay is drawing)
* Risk/reward tied to **how long you expose yourself while drawing**
* The “enemy” (Qix) moves in unpredictable, almost artistic patterns

This makes Qix feel like:

> A mix of geometry, timing, and controlled risk-taking.

---

# 4. Player Role & Fantasy

The player is effectively:

* A **cursor agent** operating on boundaries
* A **territory controller**
* A **risk manager** deciding how long to stay exposed

Emotional loop:

* Safe → Step out → Danger → Commit → Escape → Reward

---

# 5. Controls

## 5.1 Inputs

* **Joystick (4-directional)**
* **Draw Speed Button:**

  * Slow draw (safe, lower points)
  * Fast draw (high risk, high points)

## 5.2 Movement Modes

* **On Border:** safe (except Sparx)
* **Drawing (inside):** vulnerable to Qix and Fuse

---

# 6. Playfield Structure

## 6.1 Base Layout

* Large rectangular boundary
* Empty interior space
* Player starts on border

## 6.2 Territory System

* Player draws lines to split the field
* When a shape is closed:

  * One side is filled
  * The side **without the Qix** becomes captured

---

# 7. Core Gameplay Loop

1. Move along border
2. Start drawing into playfield
3. Navigate safely while exposed
4. Close shape by reconnecting to border
5. Area fills in
6. Repeat until target % reached
7. Advance to next level

---

# 8. The Qix (Primary Enemy)

## 8.1 Behavior

* Moves in erratic, abstract line patterns
* Not bound to grid or predictable paths
* Continuously animates as shifting lines

## 8.2 Function

* If Qix touches:

  * Player while drawing → death
  * Active drawing line → death

## 8.3 Design Role

* Creates **constant unpredictable pressure**
* Forces player to:

  * Watch positioning
  * Time entries carefully

---

# 9. Sparx (Edge Enemies)

## 9.1 Behavior

* Move along borders and drawn lines
* Travel clockwise or counterclockwise
* Cannot enter open space

## 9.2 Threat

* Kill player on contact (even on border)
* Block safe zones

## 9.3 Design Role

* Remove “safe” areas
* Force movement and decision-making

---

# 10. Fuse (Later Enemy)

## 10.1 Behavior

* Travels along player-drawn lines
* Appears after a delay during drawing
* Moves toward player origin point

## 10.2 Purpose

* Prevents slow, overly cautious drawing
* Forces commitment and speed

---

# 11. Drawing System (Core Mechanic)

## 11.1 Drawing States

* **Idle:** moving on border
* **Drawing:** inside field
* **Closing:** reconnecting to border

## 11.2 Drawing Types

### Slow Draw

* Safer
* Lower points

### Fast Draw

* Riskier
* Higher points
* Faster exposure window

## 11.3 Completion Rule

When player reconnects:

* Area is evaluated
* Region without Qix is filled

---

# 12. Area Capture Logic

## 12.1 Core Rule

> The side of the split NOT containing the Qix is captured.

## 12.2 Fill Behavior

* Filled region becomes:

  * Safe terrain
  * New boundary for movement

## 12.3 Strategy Implications

* Small safe cuts vs large risky cuts
* Trapping Qix into smaller zones

---

# 13. Scoring System

## 13.1 Score Sources

* Area captured
* Drawing speed (fast = more points)
* Efficiency of cuts

## 13.2 Risk/Reward

| Action    | Risk | Reward |
| --------- | ---- | ------ |
| Small cut | Low  | Low    |
| Large cut | High | High   |
| Fast draw | High | High   |
| Slow draw | Low  | Low    |

---

# 14. Level Progression

## 14.1 Win Condition

* Capture required % (≈75%)

## 14.2 Difficulty Scaling

* More Sparx
* Faster movement
* Fuse introduced
* Less safe border time
* Tighter Qix movement pressure

---

# 15. Lives & Failure

## 15.1 Death Conditions

* Touched by Qix while drawing
* Qix hits active line
* Contact with Sparx
* Fuse reaches player

## 15.2 Life System

* Limited lives
* Game continues until exhausted

---

# 16. Game Feel Requirements (CRITICAL)

To feel like Qix:

* Qix motion must feel:

  * Chaotic but not random garbage
  * Smooth and continuous
* Drawing must feel:

  * Immediate
  * Precise
* Risk must feel:

  * Visible
  * Increasing over time
* Area fill must feel:

  * Instant and satisfying

---

* position

## 17.5 TerritoryMap

* grid or polygon map
* filled vs empty regions

---

# 18. Collision Rules

Priority:

1. Qix vs draw line
2. Qix vs player
3. Sparx vs player
4. Fuse vs draw line/player
5. Draw completion validation

---

# 19. Algorithms (Key Systems)

## 19.1 Area Fill (Core Problem)

* Split polygon into two regions
* Determine which region contains Qix
* Fill the opposite region

Implementation options:

* Flood fill
* Polygon partitioning
* Grid-based occupancy

---

## 19.2 Qix Containment Check

* After line closure:

  * Determine which region contains Qix
  * Fill the other

---

## 19.3 Path Validation

* Ensure:

  * No self-crossing
  * Valid reconnection to boundary

---

# 20. Difficulty Model

Qix difficulty emerges from:

* Exposure time
* Enemy density
* Available safe zones shrinking
* Increasing player pressure

Not from speed alone.

---

# 21. Audio-Visual Identity

## 21.1 Visual Style

* Minimalist vector graphics
* Neon lines
* Abstract motion

## 21.2 Sound

* Reactive tones
* Increasing pitch with danger
* Minimalistic but tension-driven

---

# 22. Arcade Design Philosophy

Qix was designed around:

* **Short play sessions**
* **High tension per second**
* **Skill expression through risk**
* **Replayability through unpredictability**

---

# 23. QA Checklist (Faithful Clone)

Must pass:

* Qix always stays in one region after split
* Area fill never incorrectly captures Qix
* Sparx never leave borders
* Fuse forces urgency correctly
* Fast draw scores more than slow
* Capture % ends level correctly
* Player always dies if line is touched mid-draw

---

# 24. What Most Remakes Get Wrong

* Qix movement too random or too predictable
* Area fill glitches
* Drawing latency
* Weak risk/reward distinction
* Missing Fuse pressure timing

---

# 25. Final Summary

**Qix is not about drawing shapes — it’s about controlled exposure.**

The player constantly decides:

* *How far do I go?*
* *How long do I stay vulnerable?*
* *Is this cut worth the risk?*

That tension—more than visuals or scoring—is the essence of Qix.
